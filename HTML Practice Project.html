Food of the Gods

Chongqing Hot Pot Base

Chongqing hot pot base is a traditional Sichuan culinary foundation known for its distinct "ma la" (spicy and numbing) flavour profile. It is prepared by simmering beef tallow with heavy amounts of dried red chillies, Sichuan peppercorns, fermented broad bean paste, and aromatic spices. When dissolved in boiling water or bone broth, it creates the fiery, fragrant liquid used to cook meats and vegetables tableside.

Chongqing Nine-square Grid

The Chongqing Jiugongge (nine-grid) hot pot is a unique culinary tradition defined by a metal divider that splits a single pot of spicy mala broth into nine distinct sections. Originally invented by Yangtze River dock workers who shared a single pot to save money, the grid is used today as an ingenious tool for temperature control rather than separating different flavors. The layout creates three distinct cooking zones: a high-heat center square for flash-boiling quick items like beef tripe, four medium-heat side squares for simmering sliced meats, and four low-heat corner squares for slow-stewing dense ingredients like lotus root and tofu.

Chongqing Yuanyang Pot

The origins of hot pot beef stem from the evolution of ancient Chinese communal cooking (huoguo), which dates back millennia, merged with the later popularization of beef in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. While early hot pots primarily featured mutton or pork due to the historical protection of cattle for agriculture, regional culinary breakthroughs transformed beef into a staple. In Chongqing, working-class laborers along the Yangtze River popularized cooking cheap beef cuts and offal in a fiery, numbing chili-and-tallow broth to mask the tough texture. Meanwhile, in the Chaoshan region of Guangdong, chefs perfected the art of ultra-thinly slicing premium, fresh cuts of beef to be flash-cooked in a clear, delicate broth, establishing the two distinct styles that define modern beef hot pot today.

Lamb in Hot Pot

The origins of lamb in hot pot are deeply rooted in northern Chinese culinary history, heavily influenced by nomadic cultures and most famously tied to the Mongol-led Yuan dynasty and the subsequent Qing dynasty. Legend often credits Kublai Khan’s chefs with inventing thin-sliced mutton hot pot (shuan yangrou) to quickly feed rushing battlefield troops, though historical records suggest the practice of cooking mutton in communal vessels existed long before. The dish was later refined and popularized within the imperial courts of the Qing dynasty, where it became a favored winter delicacy among the elite before spreading to the general public. This Northern-style hot pot traditionally utilizes a copper pot with a central chimney, fueled by burning charcoal, in which exceptionally thin slices of tender lamb are flash-boiled in a minimalist, clear broth to emphasize the meat's natural flavor, often paired with a rich sesame paste dipping sauce.
